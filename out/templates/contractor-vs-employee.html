Contractor vs Employee Cheat Sheet

California AB5 Classification Guide for Service Businesses

CALIFORNIA LAW (AB5): Workers are presumed to be EMPLOYEES unless the hiring entity can prove ALL THREE parts of the "ABC Test." The burden of proof is on the employer. Misclassification can result in significant penalties.

The ABC Test

To classify a worker as an independent contractor in California, ALL THREE conditions must be met:

A
Free from Control & Direction

The worker is free from the control and direction of the hiring entity in performing the work, both under the contract and in fact.

Ask: Do you control HOW, WHEN, and WHERE the work is done? If yes = Employee

B
Outside Usual Course of Business

The worker performs work that is outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business.

Ask: Is this work you normally do for customers? If yes = Employee
Landscaping company using landscaper = Employee. Landscaping company using plumber = Maybe contractor.

C
Independent Trade or Business

The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business of the same nature as the work performed.

Ask: Do they have their own business, other clients, business cards, website? If no = Employee

Real-World Scenarios for Service Businesses

EMPLOYEE Landscaper you schedule for regular weekly routes

You tell them which properties to service, what days to work, provide the mower/equipment, and they only work for your company. Fails all 3 ABC tests.

EMPLOYEE Pool cleaner on your weekly customer rotation

Uses your chemicals, follows your service checklist, wears your uniform, only works for you. This is core business work with significant control = Employee.

LIKELY EMPLOYEE Painter who only works for your painting company

Even if they set some of their own hours, painting is your core business (fails B test) and they don't have an independent business (fails C test).

POSSIBLY CONTRACTOR Licensed plumber you call for specific jobs

You're a landscaping company. Plumbing is NOT your core business. The plumber has their own license, business, tools, and other clients. May pass all 3 tests.

POSSIBLY CONTRACTOR Web designer who builds your company website

You're a cleaning company, not a tech company. The designer has other clients, sets their own schedule, uses their own computer. Likely passes all 3 tests.

The "B" Test Trap for Service Businesses:
If you're a landscaping company, landscapers are doing your core business work - they're almost certainly employees. Same for cleaners at a cleaning company, painters at a painting company, etc. The "B" test is often the hardest to pass for workers doing the same type of work you offer customers.

Penalties for Misclassification

$ Back Taxes: Employer's share of FICA, FUTA, state payroll taxes
$ Unpaid Overtime: All overtime that should have been paid
$ Meal/Rest Break Penalties: 1 hour pay per violation per day
$ Wage Statement Penalties: Up to $4,000 per employee
$ Willful Misclassification: $5,000-$25,000 per violation
$ PAGA Penalties: Private attorney general lawsuits - $100-$200 per pay period per employee

When in Doubt...

Safer to Classify as Employee: The risks of misclassifying an employee as a contractor far outweigh the administrative burden of proper employment. When you're unsure, treat the worker as an employee. Consult with an employment attorney for complex situations.
